A Visual Essay

The Anatomy
of Fear

We analyzed the colors of 28,698 horror movie posters spanning a century (1920–2022) to answer one question: what does fear look like — and how has it changed?

By Juan Pablo Duque · October 2026 · 9 min read

SCROLL IF YOU DARE ↓

A horror poster has one job: scare you into a seat. But how it scares you is a fingerprint of its era. The painted monsters of the 1930s, the occult photography of the '70s, the black-and-red formula of the slasher boom, today's minimalist dread — each is a fossil of what its decade feared.

Fossils can be measured. From a database of 33,000 films we analyzed every poster with usable artwork — 28,698 of them, from 1920 to 2022 — extracting each one's dominant palette, brightness, saturation, and how much of it is the color of blood.

This is a century of fear, one pixel at a time.

Measurement 2

The Darkness

72% of this poster is near-black. Its mean brightness is L* 16.9 — less than half the average of its own year. Carpenter's one-sheet was a decade ahead of the curve the rest of the genre would follow.

Measurement 5

The Shout

The title is a billboard: one word, top of the sheet, wider than the artwork itself. Our detector flags 22% of this poster as text-like — an upper bound, and a confession: to the algorithm, a carved pumpkin's ridges read like lettering. Every measurement in this story carries that kind of noise; the trends survive it.

text-like coverage: 22% (upper bound) · the century-long trend: shouting → whisper

Measurement 6

The Monster

We asked a vision model what creature lives on this poster. Its answer: a skeleton — it reads the jack-o'-lantern's carved grin as a skull. Wrong species, right instinct: that pumpkin was designed to read as a death's-head. When the model is unsure, it says so — and we only trust it in aggregate.

Six Eras of Terror

Era palette — sample reconstruction

1920s – 1930s

Painted Monsters

Lithographed one-sheets sold the creature, not the fear — but in expressionist shadow. The surprise of the backfill: the earliest posters were darker than the atomic age that followed. The century's brightness is a mountain, and this is its first slope.

n = 632 posters (1920–49) · L* 43.7 — darker than the 1950s peak · red barely exists: 6.6%

1940s – 1950s

Atomic Pulp

Radiation, invasion, paranoia. Posters scream in exclamation marks — "SEE!" "SHOCKING!" — drenched in lurid reds and yellows borrowed from pulp magazine covers.

1950s posters are the brightest on record: L* 46.7 · dark pixels only 27% · reigning monsters: the giant creature & the alien (7.8% combined — the alien's all-time peak)

1960s – 1970s

The Occult Turn

Photography replaces paint, and the devil moves into the suburbs. Rosemary's Baby, The Exorcist: black backgrounds arrive and never leave.

The slide begins: dark pixels climb 27% → 34% · least saturated era of the century (0.35) · reigning monster: the witch — 7.1% in the '70s, her all-time peak

1980s

Black & Red

The slasher boom industrializes the formula: black void, a weapon, a scream, chrome type. The VHS shelf demanded posters that read from across a video store.

Brightness collapses 43 → 36.5 — the steepest drop on record · reigning monster: the masked killer, at his all-time peak (6.8%)

1990s – 2000s

The Desaturation

Meta-horror and torture porn: floating celebrity heads, steel blues, surgical greens. But the data busts a myth — the "cold desaturated '90s" never happened.

Myth busted: saturation holds at 0.41. 1997–99 is peak marketing horror: red at its all-time high (13.7%), the celebrity close-up (face size peaks at 9%) · the dynasty changes hands: killer → zombie (2% → 6% in one decade)

2010s – Today

Elevated Minimalism

A24 and the arthouse wave strip the poster to a single symbolic object — but look at the specimen: the era's most typical poster is a busy franchise ensemble. The elevated-horror look you remember is the exception. The average is darker, and it never let the light back in.

All-time low brightness: L* 29.8 · half of every poster (50%) near-black · faces on fewer than half (48%) · reigning monster: the ghost, 8.1% — the largest share any creature has ever held

Part V

The Vanishing Face

In the 1940s, showing a face was nearly mandatory: 85% of posters had one — the century's peak. Today, fewer than half do. And hiding inside the long retreat, one twist: the late '90s didn't add faces, it zoomed in on them. Average face size peaks in 1997 — the celebrity close-up era — at nearly twice its historic share of the sheet.

FULL RUN — n = 28,065 posters (TMDB), YuNet face detection (validated against hand-checked artwork), 5-yr rolling mean. Counts are floors: small profile/tilted faces can escape the detector.

Part VI

The Great Quieting

Two quieter revolutions, measured across every poster. First: the shout is a mountain. The quiet silent-era sheets climb to the "SEE! SHOCKING!" peak of the mid-'50s — and then horror spends seventy years lowering its voice, down to today's near-silent artwork.

FULL RUN — n = 28,065 posters (TMDB), MSER text-region proxy, 5-yr rolling mean. Trend-comparable across eras; absolute level is an upper bound.

And second: posters became obsessively symmetric. Mirror-balance climbs relentlessly and then leaps after 2010 — the measurable fingerprint of elevated horror's centered, ritual compositions.

FULL RUN — horizontal mirror similarity (0–1), 5-yr rolling mean.

Part VII

The Monster Census

We asked a vision model one question about every poster: what creature is on it? The answer is a dynastic succession — atomic monster, occult witch, masked killer, zombie — with one astonishing frame around it: the century begins and ends with the ghost. The spiritualist 1920s put spectres on 12% of posters; a hundred years of monsters later, the ghost has simply returned to the throne. Hover each line to follow one monster's fortunes.

Part VIII

The Lettering of Fear

Color wasn't the only language on the sheet — so was the title. We placed every poster on a single axis: from ornate hand-drawn display lettering — the painted, dimensional, illustrated logos of the pulp era — to minimal type, the thin, quiet words of elevated horror. The century tells one long story: fear stopped shouting its own name. In the 1940s, 63% of posters wore ornate display lettering and barely 4% were minimal. By the 2020s that has inverted — ornate down to 13%, minimal up to 31% and now the single largest register.

FULL CENTURY — n = 28,698 posters (TMDB). Registers are quintiles of an ornate↔minimal typographic axis (CLIP zero-shot on the whole poster: similarity to "ornate hand-drawn display lettering" minus "clean minimal type"). Validated against hand-ranked specimens (corr 0.81); decade trend r = −0.93. This measures the decorative character of the title treatment, not a specific typeface — a proxy, honest about its limits: reading the exact letterforms of stylized horror titles defeats OCR and font-ID models alike.

Methodology

Film list and poster images from The Movie Database (TMDB): 32,540 features tagged Horror, of which 28,065 (1950–2022) had usable artwork and were analyzed. For each poster we extracted a 5-color dominant palette (k-means in CIELAB, saturation-weighted), mean brightness (L*), mean saturation, share of near-black pixels, share of blood-red pixels, and hue-family distribution. Base dataset by Tanya Shapiro (TidyTuesday 2022). Face counts: population statistics use YuNet detection at analysis resolution (floors — small, tilted or heavily stylized faces can escape); the counts shown on individually displayed specimens are hand-verified against the artwork. This product uses the TMDB API but is not endorsed or certified by TMDB. Full code and data on GitHub. Industry context: Stephen Follows' Horror Movie Report.
