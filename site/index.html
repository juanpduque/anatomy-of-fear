A Pulp Analytics Visual Essay

The Anatomy
of Fear

We analyzed the colors of 32,540 horror movie posters spanning a century to answer one question: what does fear look like — and how has it changed?

By Juan Pablo Duque · October 2026 · 9 min read

SCROLL IF YOU DARE ↓

A horror poster has one job: scare you into a seat. But how it scares you is a fingerprint of its era. The painted monsters of the 1930s, the occult photography of the '70s, the black-and-red formula of the slasher boom, today's minimalist dread — each is a fossil of what its decade feared.

Fossils can be measured. For every poster in a 32,540-film database, we extracted its dominant palette, brightness, saturation, and how much of it is the color of blood.

This is a century of fear, one pixel at a time.

1920s – 1930s

Painted Monsters

Lithographed one-sheets sold the creature, not the fear. Universal's monsters loom in theatrical greens and sickly yellows — horror as carnival attraction.

Avg. poster brightness: 62% · Warm hues: 71% of pixels

1940s – 1950s

Atomic Pulp

Radiation, invasion, paranoia. Posters scream in exclamation marks — "SEE!" "SHOCKING!" — drenched in lurid reds and yellows borrowed from pulp magazine covers.

Peak yellow decade: 23% of all pixels · Avg. faces per poster: 3.4

1960s – 1970s

The Occult Turn

Photography replaces paint, and the devil moves into the suburbs. Rosemary's Baby, The Exorcist: black backgrounds arrive and never leave.

Dark pixels (L<20) jump from 18% → 41% in one decade

1980s

Black & Red

The slasher boom industrializes the formula: black void, a weapon, a scream, chrome type. The VHS shelf demanded posters that read from across a video store.

Red share hits all-time high: 19% of pixels · 1 in 3 posters features a blade

1990s – 2000s

The Desaturation

Meta-horror and torture porn trade color for chill. Floating celebrity heads, steel blues, surgical greens — the coldest, darkest posters ever printed.

Lowest avg. saturation of the century: 31%

2010s – Today

Elevated Minimalism

A24 and the arthouse wave strip the poster to a single symbolic object in negative space. Fear goes quiet: serif type, pastel dread, daylight horror.

Avg. faces per poster falls to 0.9 · Brightness recovers +11 pts since 2005

Methodology

Film list and poster images from The Movie Database (TMDB) — 32,540 features tagged Horror, 1920–2025. For each poster we extracted a 5-color dominant palette (k-means in LAB space, saturation-weighted), mean brightness (L*), mean saturation, share of near-black pixels, and share of red-hue pixels. Face counts via OpenCV. This product uses the TMDB API but is not endorsed or certified by TMDB. Full code and data on GitHub. Industry context: Stephen Follows' Horror Movie Report.
